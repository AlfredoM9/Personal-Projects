Los Angeles Lakers - Wikipedia
<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<p>The <b>Los Angeles Lakers</b> are an American professional <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> team based in <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">Los Angeles</a>. The Lakers compete in the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> (NBA) as a member of the league's <a href="/wiki/Western_Conference_(NBA)" title="Western Conference (NBA)">Western Conference</a> <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Division_(NBA)" title="Pacific Division (NBA)">Pacific Division</a>. The Lakers play their home games at <a href="/wiki/Staples_Center" title="Staples Center">Staples Center</a>, an arena shared with the NBA's <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers" title="Los Angeles Clippers">Los Angeles Clippers</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Sparks" title="Los Angeles Sparks">Los Angeles Sparks</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_National_Basketball_Association" title="Women's National Basketball Association">Women's National Basketball Association</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Kings" title="Los Angeles Kings">Los Angeles Kings</a> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Hockey_League" title="National Hockey League">National Hockey League</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[10]</a></sup> The Lakers are one of the most successful teams in the history of the NBA, and have won 17 NBA championships, tied with the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Boston Celtics</a> for the most in NBA history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11">[11]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The franchise began with the 1947 purchase of a disbanded team, the Detroit Gems of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_League_(United_States)" title="National Basketball League (United States)">National Basketball League</a> (NBL). The new team began playing in <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis" title="Minneapolis">Minneapolis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> calling themselves the <b>Minneapolis Lakers</b>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nicknames_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nicknames-12">[12]</a></sup> Initially a member of the NBL, the Lakers won the 1948 NBL championship before joining the rival <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Association_of_America" title="Basketball Association of America">Basketball Association of America</a>, where they would win five of the next six championships, led by star <a href="/wiki/George_Mikan" title="George Mikan">George Mikan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mike_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mike-13">[13]</a></sup> After struggling financially in the late 1950s following Mikan's retirement, they <a href="/wiki/Relocation_of_professional_sports_teams" title="Relocation of professional sports teams">relocated</a> to Los Angeles before the <a href="/wiki/1960%E2%80%9361_NBA_season" title="1960–61 NBA season">1960–61 season</a>.
</p>
<p>Led by <a href="/wiki/Naismith_Memorial_Basketball_Hall_of_Fame" title="Naismith Memorial Basketball Hall of Fame">Hall of Famers</a> <a href="/wiki/Elgin_Baylor" title="Elgin Baylor">Elgin Baylor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_West" title="Jerry West">Jerry West</a>, Los Angeles made the <a href="/wiki/NBA_Finals" title="NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> six times in the 1960s, but lost each series to the Celtics, beginning their <a href="/wiki/Celtics%E2%80%93Lakers_rivalry" title="Celtics–Lakers rivalry">long and storied rivalry</a>. In 1968, the Lakers acquired four-time <a href="/wiki/NBA_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="NBA Most Valuable Player Award">NBA Most Valuable Player</a> (MVP) <a href="/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain" title="Wilt Chamberlain">Wilt Chamberlain</a>, and won their sixth NBA title—and first in Los Angeles—in <a href="/wiki/1971%E2%80%9372_NBA_season" title="1971–72 NBA season">1972</a>, led by new head coach <a href="/wiki/Bill_Sharman" title="Bill Sharman">Bill Sharman</a>. After the retirement of West and Chamberlain, the team acquired <a href="/wiki/Kareem_Abdul-Jabbar" title="Kareem Abdul-Jabbar">Kareem Abdul-Jabbar</a>, who also won multiple MVP awards, but was unable to make the Finals in the late 1970s.
</p>
<p>The 1980s Lakers were nicknamed "<a href="/wiki/Showtime_(basketball)" title="Showtime (basketball)">Showtime</a>" due to their fast break-offense led by <a href="/wiki/Magic_Johnson" title="Magic Johnson">Magic Johnson</a>. The team won five championships in a nine-year span, and contained Hall of Famers Johnson, Abdul-Jabbar, and <a href="/wiki/James_Worthy" title="James Worthy">James Worthy</a>, and was led by Hall of Fame coach <a href="/wiki/Pat_Riley" title="Pat Riley">Pat Riley</a>. After Abdul-Jabbar and Johnson retired, the team struggled in the early 1990s, before acquiring <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O'Neal">Shaquille O'Neal</a> and <a href="/wiki/Kobe_Bryant" title="Kobe Bryant">Kobe Bryant</a> in 1996. With the duo, who were led by another Hall of Fame coach <a href="/wiki/Phil_Jackson" title="Phil Jackson">Phil Jackson</a>, the team won three consecutive titles between <a href="/wiki/2000_NBA_Finals" title="2000 NBA Finals">2000</a> to <a href="/wiki/2002_NBA_Finals" title="2002 NBA Finals">2002</a>, securing the franchise its second "<a href="/wiki/Three-peat" title="Three-peat">three-peat</a>". Led by Bryant, the Lakers won two more championships in <a href="/wiki/2009_NBA_Finals" title="2009 NBA Finals">2009</a> and <a href="/wiki/2010_NBA_Finals" title="2010 NBA Finals">2010</a>, but failed to regain their former glory in the following decade. Led by <a href="/wiki/LeBron_James" title="LeBron James">LeBron James</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Davis" title="Anthony Davis">Anthony Davis</a>, the Lakers claimed their 17th championship in the <a href="/wiki/2020_NBA_Finals" title="2020 NBA Finals">2020 NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[14]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers hold the record for <a href="/wiki/List_of_National_Basketball_Association_longest_winning_streaks" title="List of National Basketball Association longest winning streaks">NBA's longest winning streak</a>, 33 straight games, set during the 1971–72 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[15]</a></sup> Twenty-six Hall of Famers have played for Los Angeles, while four have coached the team. Four Lakers—Abdul-Jabbar, Johnson, O'Neal, and Bryant—have won the NBA MVP Award for a total of eight awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[16]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers' franchise began in 1947 when <a href="/wiki/Ben_Berger" title="Ben Berger">Ben Berger</a> and <a href="/wiki/Morris_Chalfen" title="Morris Chalfen">Morris Chalfen</a> of <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">Minnesota</a> purchased the recently disbanded Detroit Gems<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[17]</a></sup> of the <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_League_(United_States)" title="National Basketball League (United States)">National Basketball League</a> (NBL) for $15,000 from Gems owner Maury Winston.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18">[18]</a></sup> Minneapolis sportswriter <a href="/wiki/Sid_Hartman" title="Sid Hartman">Sid Hartman</a> played a key behind the scenes role in helping put together the deal and later the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[19]</a></sup> Inspired by Minnesota's nickname, "Land of 10,000 Lakes", the team christened themselves the Lakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Nicknames_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-The_Nicknames-12">[12]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[20]</a></sup> Hartman helped them hire <a href="/wiki/John_Kundla" title="John Kundla">John Kundla</a> from <a href="/wiki/University_of_St._Thomas_(Minnesota)" title="University of St. Thomas (Minnesota)">College of St. Thomas</a>, to be their first head coach, by meeting with him and selling him on the team.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[21]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[22]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers had a solid roster, which featured forward <a href="/wiki/Jim_Pollard" title="Jim Pollard">Jim Pollard</a>, playmaker <a href="/wiki/Herm_Schaefer" title="Herm Schaefer">Herm Schaefer</a>, and center <a href="/wiki/George_Mikan" title="George Mikan">George Mikan</a>, who became the most dominant player in the NBL.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minneapolis_Lakers_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minneapolis_Lakers-23">[23]</a></sup> In their first season, they led the league with a 43–17 record, later winning the NBL Championship that season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[24]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In 1948, the Lakers moved from the NBL to the <a href="/wiki/Basketball_Association_of_America" title="Basketball Association of America">Basketball Association of America</a> (BAA), and Mikan's 28.3 point per game (ppg) scoring average set a BAA record. In the <a href="/wiki/1949_BAA_Finals" title="1949 BAA Finals">1949 BAA Finals</a> they won the championship, beating the <a href="/wiki/Washington_Capitols" title="Washington Capitols">Washington Capitols</a> four games to two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[25]</a></sup> The following season, the team improved to 51–17, repeating as champions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[26]</a></sup> In the 1950–51 season, Mikan won his third straight scoring title at 28.4 ppg and the Lakers went 44–24 to win their second straight division title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mikan_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-mikan-27">[27]</a></sup> One of those games, a 19–18 loss against the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne_Pistons" title="Fort Wayne Pistons">Fort Wayne Pistons</a>, became infamous as the lowest scoring game in NBA history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[28]</a></sup> In the playoffs, they defeated the <a href="/wiki/Indianapolis_Olympians" title="Indianapolis Olympians">Indianapolis Olympians</a> in three games but lost to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rochester_Royals" title="Rochester Royals">Rochester Royals</a> in the next round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minneapolis_Lakers_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Minneapolis_Lakers-23">[23]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/1951%E2%80%9352_NBA_season" title="1951–52 NBA season">1951–52 season</a>, the Lakers won 40 games, finishing second in their division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup> They faced the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York Knicks</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1952_NBA_Finals" title="1952 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>, which they won in seven games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La46_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-La46-30">[30]</a></sup> In the <a href="/wiki/1952%E2%80%9353_NBA_season" title="1952–53 NBA season">1952–53</a> season, Mikan led the NBA in rebounding, averaging 14.4 rebounds per game (rpg), and was named MVP of the <a href="/wiki/1953_NBA_All-Star_Game" title="1953 NBA All-Star Game">1953 NBA All-Star Game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mikan_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-mikan-27">[27]</a></sup> After a 48–22 regular season, the Lakers defeated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fort_Wayne_Pistons" title="Fort Wayne Pistons">Fort Wayne Pistons</a> in the Western playoffs to advance to <a href="/wiki/1953_NBA_Finals" title="1953 NBA Finals">the NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La46_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-La46-30">[30]</a></sup> They then defeated the New York Knicks to win their second straight championship.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La50_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-La50-31">[31]</a></sup> Though Lakers star George Mikan suffered from knee problems throughout the <a href="/wiki/1953%E2%80%9354_NBA_season" title="1953–54 NBA season">1953–54</a> season, he was still able to average 18 ppg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La49_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-La49-32">[32]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Lovellette" title="Clyde Lovellette">Clyde Lovellette</a>, who was drafted in <a href="/wiki/1952_NBA_draft" title="1952 NBA draft">1952</a>, helped the team win the Western Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La49_32-1"><a href="#cite_note-La49-32">[32]</a></sup> The team won its third straight championship in the 1950s and fifth in six seasons when it defeated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syracuse_Nationals" title="Syracuse Nationals">Syracuse Nationals</a> in seven games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La50_31-1"><a href="#cite_note-La50-31">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Following Mikan's retirement in the 1954 off-season, the Lakers struggled but still managed to win 40 games. Although they defeated the Rochester Royals in the first round of the playoffs, they were defeated by the Fort Wayne Pistons in the semifinals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[33]</a></sup> Although they had losing records the next two seasons, they made the playoffs each year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup> Mikan came back for the last half of the <a href="/wiki/1955%E2%80%9356_NBA_season" title="1955–56 NBA season">1955–56 season</a>, but struggled and retired for good after the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[34]</a></sup> Led by Lovellette's 20.6 points and 13.5 rebounds, they advanced to the Conference Finals in 1956–57. The Lakers had one of the worst seasons in team history in 1957–58 when they won a league-low 19 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[35]</a></sup> They had hired Mikan, who had been the team's general manager for the previous two seasons, as head coach to replace Kundla. Mikan was fired in January when the team was 9–30, and Kundla was rehired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-2"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers earned the top pick in the <a href="/wiki/1958_NBA_draft" title="1958 NBA draft">1958 NBA draft</a> and used it to select <a href="/wiki/Elgin_Baylor" title="Elgin Baylor">Elgin Baylor</a>. Baylor, who was named <a href="/wiki/NBA_Rookie_of_the_Year_Award" title="NBA Rookie of the Year Award">NBA Rookie of the Year</a> and co-MVP of the <a href="/wiki/1959_NBA_All-Star_Game" title="1959 NBA All-Star Game">1959 NBA All-Star Game</a>, averaged 24.9 ppg and 15.0 rpg helping the Lakers improve to second in their division despite a 33–39 record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[37]</a></sup> After upsetting the Hawks in six games in the division finals, they returned to the NBA Finals, but were swept by the Celtics, beginning their long rivalry.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[38]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In their <a href="/wiki/1959%E2%80%9360_NBA_season" title="1959–60 NBA season">last year</a> in Minneapolis, the Lakers went 25–50. On January 18, 1960, the team was coming off a loss and traveling to St. Louis when their plane crash-landed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41">[41]</a></sup> Snow storms had driven the pilot 150 miles off course when he was forced to land in a cornfield. No one was hurt.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42">[42]</a></sup> Their record earned them the number two pick in the <a href="/wiki/1960_NBA_draft" title="1960 NBA draft">1960 NBA draft</a>. The team selected <a href="/wiki/Jerry_West" title="Jerry West">Jerry West</a> from <a href="/wiki/West_Virginia_Mountaineers_men%27s_basketball" title="West Virginia Mountaineers men's basketball">West Virginia University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43">[43]</a></sup> During the 1960 off-season, the Lakers became the NBA's first West Coast team when owner <a href="/wiki/Bob_Short" title="Bob Short">Bob Short</a> decided to move the team to Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Lakers_#_10_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Lakers_#_10-44">[44]</a></sup> Led by Baylor's 34.8 ppg and 19.8 rpg, Los Angeles won 11 more than the year before in West's first season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1961_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1961-45">[45]</a></sup> On November 15 that season, Baylor set a new NBA scoring record when he scored 71 points in a victory against the New York Knicks while grabbing 25 rebounds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46">[46]</a></sup> In doing so, Baylor broke his own NBA record of 64 points. Despite a losing record, the Lakers made <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1961_NBA_Playoffs" title="1961 NBA Playoffs">the playoffs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-3"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup> They came within two points of the <a href="/wiki/1961_NBA_Finals" title="1961 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> when they lost in game seven of their second round series against St. Louis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47">[47]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Led by Baylor and West at 38.3 and 30.8 ppg respectively,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1962_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1962-48">[48]</a></sup> the Lakers improved to 54–26 in <a href="/wiki/1961%E2%80%9362_NBA_season" title="1961–62 NBA season">1961–62</a>, and made the <a href="/wiki/1962_NBA_Finals" title="1962 NBA Finals">finals</a>. In a game five victory, Baylor grabbed 22 rebounds and set the still-standing NBA record for points in a finals game with 61,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49">[49]</a></sup> despite fouling out of the game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50">[50]</a></sup> The Lakers, however, lost to the Celtics by three points in overtime of game seven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1962_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1962-48">[48]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Selvy" title="Frank Selvy">Frank Selvy</a>, after making two jumpers in the final 40 seconds to tie the game,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51">[51]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selvy_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-Selvy-52">[52]</a></sup> missed a potential game-winning 18 foot <a href="/wiki/Jump_shot_(basketball)" title="Jump shot (basketball)">jump shot</a> in regulation, a miss which he said in June 2010 still haunted him more than 40 years later.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selvy_52-1"><a href="#cite_note-Selvy-52">[52]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Los Angeles won 53 games in <a href="/wiki/1962%E2%80%9363_NBA_season" title="1962–63 NBA season">1962–63</a>, behind Baylor's 34.0 ppg and West's 27.1 ppg<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1963_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1963-53">[53]</a></sup> but lost in the NBA Finals in six games to the Celtics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1963_53-1"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1963-53">[53]</a></sup> After falling to 42–38 and losing in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1964_NBA_Playoffs" title="1964 NBA Playoffs">1964 NBA Playoffs</a> to the Hawks, the team won 49 games in 1964–65. The Lakers surged past the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baltimore_Bullets_(1963%E2%80%9373)" title="Baltimore Bullets (1963–73)">Baltimore Bullets</a> in the division finals, behind West's record-setting 46.3 ppg in the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54">[54]</a></sup> They lost again to Celtics in the Finals however, this time in five games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55">[55]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Los Angeles lost in the finals to Boston in seven games again in 1966, this time by two points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56">[56]</a></sup> Down by 16 entering the fourth quarter, and 10 with a minute and a half to go, the Lakers mounted a furious rally in the closing moments, which fell just short.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57">[57]</a></sup> After dropping to 36 wins and losing in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1967_NBA_Playoffs" title="1967 NBA Playoffs">1967 NBA Playoffs</a>, they lost in the finals to the Celtics again <a href="/wiki/1968_NBA_Finals" title="1968 NBA Finals">in 1968</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-4"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup> Los Angeles moved to a brand-new arena, <a href="/wiki/The_Forum_(Inglewood,_California)" title="The Forum (Inglewood, California)">The Forum</a>, in 1967, after playing seven seasons at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Sports_Arena" title="Los Angeles Memorial Sports Arena">Los Angeles Memorial Sports Arena</a>.
</p>
<p>On July 9, 1968, the team acquired <a href="/wiki/Wilt_Chamberlain" title="Wilt Chamberlain">Wilt Chamberlain</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia_76ers" title="Philadelphia 76ers">Philadelphia 76ers</a> for Darrell Imhoff, <a href="/wiki/Archie_Clark_(basketball)" title="Archie Clark (basketball)">Archie Clark</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Chambers" title="Jerry Chambers">Jerry Chambers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59">[59]</a></sup> In his first season as a Laker, Chamberlain set a team record by averaging a league-leading 21.1 rpg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chamberlain_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-chamberlain-60">[60]</a></sup> West, Baylor, and Chamberlain each averaged over 20 points, and Los Angeles won their division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL1969_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-LAL1969-61">[61]</a></sup> The Lakers and Celtics again met in the <a href="/wiki/1969_NBA_Finals" title="1969 NBA Finals">finals</a>, and Los Angeles had home court advantage against Boston for the first time in their rivalry. They won the first game behind Jerry West's 53 points,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62">[62]</a></sup> and had a 3–2 lead after five.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63">[63]</a></sup> Boston won the series in seven games however,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64">[64]</a></sup> and earned their 11th NBA Championship in 13 seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65">[65]</a></sup> West was named the first-ever <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Finals_MVP_Award" title="NBA Finals MVP Award">Finals MVP</a>; this remains the only time that a member of the losing team has won the award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66">[66]</a></sup> In <a href="/wiki/1969%E2%80%9370_NBA_season" title="1969–70 NBA season">1970</a>, West won his first scoring title at 31.2 ppg, the team returned to the <a href="/wiki/1970_NBA_Finals" title="1970 NBA Finals">finals</a>, and for the first time in 16 years, they did not have to face the Celtics; instead playing the New York Knicks, who defeated them 4–3.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBA_Finals:_All-Time_Champions_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBA_Finals:_All-Time_Champions-67">[67]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1970_NBA_Finals_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-1970_NBA_Finals-68">[68]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1970%E2%80%9371_NBA_season" title="1970–71 NBA season">next season</a> the Lakers were defeated by the <a href="/wiki/Milwaukee_Bucks" title="Milwaukee Bucks">Milwaukee Bucks</a>, led by future Laker Lew Alcindor (now known as <a href="/wiki/Kareem_Abdul-Jabbar" title="Kareem Abdul-Jabbar">Kareem Abdul-Jabbar</a>) in the Western Conference Finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69">[69]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1971%E2%80%9372_NBA_season" title="1971–72 NBA season">1971–72 season</a> brought several changes. Owner <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kent_Cooke" title="Jack Kent Cooke">Jack Kent Cooke</a> brought in <a href="/wiki/Bill_Sharman" title="Bill Sharman">Bill Sharman</a> as head coach,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#15_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#15-70">[70]</a></sup> and Elgin Baylor announced his retirement early in the season after realizing that his legs were not healthy enough.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#15_70-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#15-70">[70]</a></sup> Sharman increased the team's discipline.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71"><a href="#cite_note-71">[71]</a></sup> He introduced the concept of the <a href="/wiki/Shootaround" title="Shootaround">shootaround</a>, where players would arrive at the arena early in the morning before a game to practice shots.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72">[72]</a></sup> They won 14 straight games in November and all 16 games played in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#15_70-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#15-70">[70]</a></sup> They won three straight to open the year of 1972 but on January 9, the Milwaukee Bucks ended their winning streak by defeating the Lakers, 120–104.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-73"><a href="#cite_note-73">[73]</a></sup> By winning 33 straight games, Los Angeles set a record for longest winning streak of any team in American professional sports.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-74"><a href="#cite_note-74">[74]</a></sup> The Lakers won 69 games that season, which stood as the NBA record for 24 years until the Chicago Bulls won 72 games in 1995–96.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-75"><a href="#cite_note-75">[75]</a></sup> Chamberlain averaged a low 14.8 points but led the league in rebounding at 19.2 a game.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballref72_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-ballref72-76">[76]</a></sup> West's 9.7 assists per game (apg) led the league, he also averaged more than 25 points, and was named MVP of the <a href="/wiki/1972_NBA_All-Star_Game" title="1972 NBA All-Star Game">1972 NBA All-Star Game</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ballref72_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-ballref72-76">[76]</a></sup> The team failed to score 100 points just once all year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77">[77]</a></sup> and at the end of the season, Bill Sharman was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Coach_of_the_Year" title="NBA Coach of the Year">Coach of the Year</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78">[78]</a></sup> The Lakers went on to reach the <a href="/wiki/1972_NBA_Finals" title="1972 NBA Finals">finals</a> against the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York Knicks</a> where they would avenge their 1970 <a href="/wiki/1970_NBA_Finals" title="1970 NBA Finals">finals</a> loss by defeating them 4 games to 1. Chamberlain tallied 24 points and 29 rebounds in game five and won the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_Russell_NBA_Finals_Most_Valuable_Player_Award" title="Bill Russell NBA Finals Most Valuable Player Award">NBA Finals Most Valuable Player Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-79"><a href="#cite_note-79">[79]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-80"><a href="#cite_note-80">[80]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers won 60 games in the <a href="/wiki/1972%E2%80%9373_NBA_season" title="1972–73 NBA season">1972–73 NBA season</a>, and took another Pacific Division title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#16_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#16-81">[81]</a></sup> Wilt Chamberlain, playing in his final season, again led the league in rebounding and set the still standing NBA record for field-goal percentage at 72.7%.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#16_81-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#16-81">[81]</a></sup> The team defeated the Chicago Bulls in seven games in the conference semifinals, then the <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> in five in the Western Division Finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#16_81-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#16-81">[81]</a></sup> They played the New York Knicks in the <a href="/wiki/1973_NBA_Finals" title="1973 NBA Finals">1973 NBA Finals</a>. Los Angeles took the first game by three points, but New York won the series in five games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-82"><a href="#cite_note-82">[82]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/1973%E2%80%9374_NBA_season" title="1973–74 NBA season">1973–74 season</a>, the team was hampered by the loss of West, who played only 31 games before his legs gave out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#16_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#16-83">[83]</a></sup> Goodrich, averaging 25.3 points, helped the team to a late-season surge.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#16_83-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#16-83">[83]</a></sup> Trailing the Golden State Warriors by three games with seven left to play, the Lakers rallied to finish 47–35 and win the Pacific Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#16_83-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#16-83">[83]</a></sup> They made the playoffs but managed just one win against Milwaukee in the conference semifinals. Following the season, West retired due to contract disagreements with Cooke, and filed a suit for unpaid back wages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-84"><a href="#cite_note-84">[84]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>After missing the playoffs in the <a href="/wiki/1974%E2%80%9375_NBA_season" title="1974–75 NBA season">1974–75 season</a>, the Lakers acquired <a href="/wiki/Kareem_Abdul-Jabbar" title="Kareem Abdul-Jabbar">Kareem Abdul-Jabbar</a>, who had won three league MVP's by that time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85">[85]</a></sup> Abdul-Jabbar wanted out of Milwaukee, demanding a trade to either New York or Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86">[86]</a></sup> He was traded for <a href="/wiki/Elmore_Smith" title="Elmore Smith">Elmore Smith</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Winters" title="Brian Winters">Brian Winters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Junior_Bridgeman" title="Junior Bridgeman">Junior Bridgeman</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Meyers_(basketball)" title="David Meyers (basketball)">Dave Meyers</a>. Abdul-Jabbar had his fourth MVP season in <a href="/wiki/1975%E2%80%9376_NBA_season" title="1975–76 NBA season">1975–76</a>, leading the league in rebounding, blocked shots, and minutes played.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#17_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#17-87">[87]</a></sup> The Lakers struggled in January, going 3–10, and finished out of the playoffs at 40–42.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#17_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#17-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>West and Cooke settled their differences—and the former Laker's lawsuit—and Cooke hired him to replace Sharman as the team's coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88">[88]</a></sup> West became upset, however, when Cooke refused to spend the money necessary to acquire forward <a href="/wiki/Julius_Erving" title="Julius Erving">Julius Erving</a>, who the Nets were selling.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-89"><a href="#cite_note-89">[89]</a></sup> Behind another MVP season from Abdul-Jabbar, Los Angeles won the Pacific Division, finishing the 1976–77 season a league-best 53–29.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-90"><a href="#cite_note-90">[90]</a></sup> They defeated the Warriors in a seven-game series to open the postseason before being swept by Portland in the Western Conference Finals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#17_87-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#17-87">[87]</a></sup> During the off-season, Los Angeles picked up <a href="/wiki/Jamaal_Wilkes" title="Jamaal Wilkes">Jamaal Wilkes</a> from Golden State and signed first-round draft pick <a href="/wiki/Norm_Nixon" title="Norm Nixon">Norm Nixon</a>.
</p>
<p>In the first two minutes of the first game of the 1977–78 season, Abdul-Jabbar punched Bucks center <a href="/wiki/Kent_Benson" title="Kent Benson">Kent Benson</a> for an overly aggressive elbow and broke his hand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-91"><a href="#cite_note-91">[91]</a></sup> Two months later, a healthy Abdul-Jabbar got into an altercation with <a href="/wiki/Houston_Rockets" title="Houston Rockets">Houston Rockets</a> center <a href="/wiki/Kevin_Kunnert" title="Kevin Kunnert">Kevin Kunnert</a> after a rebound. The team's starting power forward, <a href="/wiki/Kermit_Washington" title="Kermit Washington">Kermit Washington</a>, who was averaging 11.5 points and 11.2 rebounds,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-92"><a href="#cite_note-92">[92]</a></sup> entered the fight, and when <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Tomjanovich" title="Rudy Tomjanovich">Rudy Tomjanovich</a> ran in from the bench to break up the action, Washington punched him in the face.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93">[93]</a></sup> Tomjanovich nearly died from the punch, suffering a fractured skull and other facial injuries, which prematurely ended his playing career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94">[94]</a></sup> Washington, who stated that he assumed Tomjanovich was a combatant, was suspended for two months by the NBA, and released by the Lakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95">[95]</a></sup> The team won 45 games despite being down a starter in Washington and not having Abdul-Jabbar for nearly two months, but lost in the first round of the playoffs to Seattle.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96">[96]</a></sup> During the 1978–79 season, the team posted a 47–35 record but lost to the SuperSonics in the semifinal round of the playoffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#17_87-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#17-87">[87]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1979_NBA_draft" title="1979 NBA draft">1979 NBA draft</a>, Los Angeles selected <span class="nowrap">6-foot, 9-inch</span> <a href="/wiki/Point_guard" title="Point guard">point guard</a> <a href="/wiki/Magic_Johnson" title="Magic Johnson">Magic Johnson</a> from <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_Spartans_men%27s_basketball" title="Michigan State Spartans men's basketball">Michigan State</a> with the first overall pick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-97"><a href="#cite_note-97">[97]</a></sup> It took Johnson's teammates time to acclimate themselves to his passing ability, as his "no-look" passes often caught them unaware. Once they adjusted, his passing became a key part of Los Angeles' offense.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-98"><a href="#cite_note-98">[98]</a></sup> The Lakers won 60 games in Johnson's rookie year, and defeated the <a href="/wiki/1979%E2%80%9380_Philadelphia_76ers_season" title="1979–80 Philadelphia 76ers season">Philadelphia 76ers</a> in six games in the <a href="/wiki/1980_NBA_Finals" title="1980 NBA Finals">1980 NBA Finals</a>. Johnson won the Finals MVP award, after starting at center for the injured Abdul-Jabbar in game six, and tallying 42 points, 15 rebounds, and seven assists.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-99"><a href="#cite_note-99">[99]</a></sup> The team fell off in the <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1980–81 Los Angeles Lakers season">1980–81 season</a>, though, as the Lakers lost Johnson for most of the season to a knee injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup> The team turned in a 54–28 record and finished second behind the <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1980–81 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix Suns</a> in the Pacific Division.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1980%E2%80%9381_Houston_Rockets_season" title="1980–81 Houston Rockets season">Rockets</a>, led by <a href="/wiki/Moses_Malone" title="Moses Malone">Moses Malone</a>, defeated Los Angeles in the first round of the playoffs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Early in the <a href="/wiki/1981%E2%80%9382_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1981–82 Los Angeles Lakers season">1981–82 season</a>, Johnson complained to the media about head coach <a href="/wiki/Paul_Westhead" title="Paul Westhead">Paul Westhead</a> and demanded a trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-101"><a href="#cite_note-101">[101]</a></sup> Westhead was fired shortly after Johnson's criticisms, and although Lakers' owner <a href="/wiki/Jerry_Buss" title="Jerry Buss">Jerry Buss</a> stated that Johnson's comments did not factor into the decision, Johnson was vilified by the national media and booed both on the road and at home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-102"><a href="#cite_note-102">[102]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-103"><a href="#cite_note-103">[103]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-104"><a href="#cite_note-104">[104]</a></sup> Buss promoted assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Pat_Riley" title="Pat Riley">Pat Riley</a> to "co-head coach" with Jerry West (although West considered himself Riley's assistant) on November 19 and the team won 17 of its next 20 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buss_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buss-105">[105]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-106"><a href="#cite_note-106">[106]</a></sup> Nicknamed "<a href="/wiki/Showtime_(basketball)" title="Showtime (basketball)">Showtime</a>" due to the team's new Johnson-led fast break-offense, the Lakers won the Pacific Division title and swept both the <a href="/wiki/1981%E2%80%9382_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1981–82 Phoenix Suns season">Suns</a> and <a href="/wiki/1981%E2%80%9382_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="1981–82 San Antonio Spurs season">Spurs</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1982_NBA_Playoffs" title="1982 NBA Playoffs">1982 playoffs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-107"><a href="#cite_note-107">[107]</a></sup> Los Angeles stretched its postseason winning streak to nine games by taking the first contest of the <a href="/wiki/1982_NBA_Finals" title="1982 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> from the <a href="/wiki/1981%E2%80%9382_Philadelphia_76ers_season" title="1981–82 Philadelphia 76ers season">76ers</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-108"><a href="#cite_note-108">[108]</a></sup> The team won the Finals 4–2 to finish a 12–2 playoff run.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#19_100-5"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#19-100">[100]</a></sup> On draft night in <a href="/wiki/1982_NBA_draft" title="1982 NBA draft">1982</a>, the Lakers had the first overall pick (the result of a trade with Cleveland midway through the 1979–80 season, when the Lakers had sent Don Ford and a 1980 first-round pick to the Cavaliers for Butch Lee and their 1982 selection) and selected <a href="/wiki/James_Worthy" title="James Worthy">James Worthy</a> from <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina_Tar_Heels_men%27s_basketball" title="North Carolina Tar Heels men's basketball">North Carolina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Worthy_Draft_Pick_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-A_Worthy_Draft_Pick-109">[109]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1982–83 Los Angeles Lakers season">Lakers</a> won the Pacific Division at 58–24, but Worthy suffered a leg injury in the last week of the season and missed the rest of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La222_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-La222-110">[110]</a></sup> Nevertheless, they advanced to play <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_Philadelphia_76ers_season" title="1982–83 Philadelphia 76ers season">Philadelphia</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1983_NBA_Finals" title="1983 NBA Finals">1983 NBA Finals</a> after defeating <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1982–83 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a> and <a href="/wiki/1982%E2%80%9383_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="1982–83 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La222_110-1"><a href="#cite_note-La222-110">[110]</a></sup> The Sixers, however, won the series and the championship in four games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-A_Worthy_Draft_Pick_109-1"><a href="#cite_note-A_Worthy_Draft_Pick-109">[109]</a></sup> After the season West replaced Sharman as the team's GM.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111">[111]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1983%E2%80%9384_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1983–84 Los Angeles Lakers season">1983–84 season</a>, Los Angeles went 54–28, and played <a href="/wiki/1983%E2%80%9384_Boston_Celtics_season" title="1983–84 Boston Celtics season">Boston</a> in the <a href="/wiki/1984_NBA_Finals" title="1984 NBA Finals">Finals</a> for the first time since <a href="/wiki/1969_NBA_Finals" title="1969 NBA Finals">1969</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBAF_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-NBAF-112">[112]</a></sup> The Lakers won two of the first three games. However, <a href="/wiki/Kevin_McHale_(basketball)" title="Kevin McHale (basketball)">Kevin McHale</a>'s hard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clothesline_from_hell" title="Clothesline from hell">clothesline</a> foul of Lakers forward <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Rambis" title="Kurt Rambis">Kurt Rambis</a> on a fast break is credited as a turning point of the series.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113">[113]</a></sup> Boston won three of the next four to win the title and send Los Angeles's record to 0–8 in Finals series against the Celtics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NBAF_112-1"><a href="#cite_note-NBAF-112">[112]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Using the past year's Finals defeat as motivation, <a href="/wiki/1984%E2%80%9385_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1984–85 Los Angeles Lakers season">the team</a> won the Pacific Division for the fourth straight year and lost just two games in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1985_NBA_Playoffs" title="1985 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference playoffs</a>. In the <a href="/wiki/1985_NBA_Finals" title="1985 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>, the <a href="/wiki/1984%E2%80%9385_Boston_Celtics_season" title="1984–85 Boston Celtics season">Celtics</a> were again the Lakers' final hurdle. Los Angeles lost game one of the NBA Finals by a score of 148–114, in what is remembered as the "Memorial Day Massacre".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kareem,_Lakers_Conquer_the_Celtic_Mystique_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kareem,_Lakers_Conquer_the_Celtic_Mystique-114">[114]</a></sup> The Lakers, behind 38-year-old Finals MVP Abdul-Jabbar, recovered to defeat the Celtics in six games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-115"><a href="#cite_note-115">[115]</a></sup> The team won the title in the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Garden" title="Boston Garden">Boston Garden</a>, becoming the only visiting team to ever win an NBA championship there.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kareem,_Lakers_Conquer_the_Celtic_Mystique_114-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kareem,_Lakers_Conquer_the_Celtic_Mystique-114">[114]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1985%E2%80%9386_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1985–86 Los Angeles Lakers season">1985–86 season</a>, the Lakers started 24–3 and went on to win 62 games and their fifth straight division title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_games_in_the_1985–1986_NBA_season_and_playoffs_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_games_in_the_1985–1986_NBA_season_and_playoffs-117">[117]</a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/1985%E2%80%9386_Houston_Rockets_season" title="1985–86 Houston Rockets season">Rockets</a>, however, defeated the Lakers in five games in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1986_NBA_Playoffs" title="1986 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference Finals</a>. Houston won the series when <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Sampson" title="Ralph Sampson">Ralph Sampson</a> hit a 20-foot jumper as time expired in game five at The Forum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_games_in_the_1985–1986_NBA_season_and_playoffs_117-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_games_in_the_1985–1986_NBA_season_and_playoffs-117">[117]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Prior to the <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1986–87 Los Angeles Lakers season">1986–87 season</a>, the Lakers moved <a href="/wiki/A._C._Green" title="A. C. Green">A. C. Green</a> into the starting lineup, and acquired <a href="/wiki/Mychal_Thompson" title="Mychal Thompson">Mychal Thompson</a> from the Spurs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#23_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#23-118">[118]</a></sup> Johnson won his first career MVP Award while leading the Lakers to a 65–17 record,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#23_118-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#23-118">[118]</a></sup> and Michael Cooper was named NBA Defensive Player of the Year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#23_118-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#23-118">[118]</a></sup> Before the season, Riley had made the decision to shift the focus of the offense to Johnson over the 40-year-old Abdul-Jabbar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119">[119]</a></sup> The Lakers advanced to the <a href="/wiki/1987_NBA_Finals" title="1987 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> by sweeping the <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Denver_Nuggets_season" title="1986–87 Denver Nuggets season">Nuggets</a>, defeating the <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Golden_State_Warriors_season" title="1986–87 Golden State Warriors season">Warriors</a> in five games, and sweeping the <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Seattle_SuperSonics_season" title="1986–87 Seattle SuperSonics season">SuperSonics</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1987_NBA_Playoffs" title="1987 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-120"><a href="#cite_note-120">[120]</a></sup> The Lakers defeated <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Boston_Celtics_season" title="1986–87 Boston Celtics season">Boston</a> in the first two games of the Finals, and the teams split the next four games, giving Los Angeles their second championship in three seasons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#23_118-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#23-118">[118]</a></sup> The series was highlighted by Johnson's running "baby hook" shot to win game four at Boston Garden with two seconds remaining.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121">[121]</a></sup> Johnson was named the NBA Finals MVP, in addition to regular season MVP.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122">[122]</a></sup> At the Lakers' championship celebration in Los Angeles, coach Riley brashly declared that Los Angeles would repeat as NBA champions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_History_#23_118-4"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_History_#23-118">[118]</a></sup> which no team had done since the <a href="/wiki/1968%E2%80%9369_Boston_Celtics_season" title="1968–69 Boston Celtics season">1968–69 Boston Celtics</a>.
</p>
<p>Looking to make good on Riley's promise in the <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1987–88 Los Angeles Lakers season">1987–88 season</a>, the Lakers took their seventh consecutive Pacific Division title with a 62–20 record. They swept the <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="1987–88 San Antonio Spurs season">Spurs</a> in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1988_NBA_Playoffs" title="1988 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference Finals</a> before pulling out a tough seven-game series win over the <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_Utah_Jazz_season" title="1987–88 Utah Jazz season">Utah Jazz</a> led by youngsters <a href="/wiki/Karl_Malone" title="Karl Malone">Karl Malone</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Stockton" title="John Stockton">John Stockton</a>. A seven-game Western Conference finals win over the <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_Dallas_Mavericks_season" title="1987–88 Dallas Mavericks season">Dallas Mavericks</a> propelled the Lakers to the <a href="/wiki/1988_NBA_Finals" title="1988 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> once again. In their seventh trip to the Finals in nine years, they met the <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_Detroit_Pistons_season" title="1987–88 Detroit Pistons season">Detroit Pistons</a>. Los Angeles would take the series in seven games, and James Worthy's game seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triple-double" title="Triple-double">triple-double</a> earned him a Finals MVP award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123">[123]</a></sup> The win marked their fifth title in nine years, but would also mark their last title until <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1999–2000 Los Angeles Lakers season">2000</a>.
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1988–89 Los Angeles Lakers season">1988–89 season</a>, Los Angeles won 57 games and their eighth consecutive Pacific Division crown. They swept the through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1989_NBA_Playoffs" title="1989 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a> defeating <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1988–89 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Seattle_SuperSonics_season" title="1988–89 Seattle SuperSonics season">Seattle</a>, and <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1988–89 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix</a>. In eighth trip to the <a href="/wiki/1989_NBA_Finals" title="1989 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> in 10 years, they once again faced the <a href="/wiki/1988%E2%80%9389_Detroit_Pistons_season" title="1988–89 Detroit Pistons season">Detroit Pistons</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124">[124]</a></sup> Hampered by injuries to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byron_Scott_(basketball)" title="Byron Scott (basketball)">Byron Scott</a> and Johnson, the Lakers were swept by Detroit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125">[125]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Following the 1989 Finals, on June 28, 1989, after 20 professional seasons, Kareem Abdul-Jabbar announced his retirement. <a href="/wiki/1989%E2%80%9390_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1989–90 Los Angeles Lakers season">The Lakers</a> still cruised through the Pacific Division, winning their ninth consecutive division crown with a 63–19 record. However, after beating the <a href="/wiki/1989%E2%80%9390_Houston_Rockets_season" title="1989–90 Houston Rockets season">Rockets</a> in the first round, they lost four games to one in the second round of the playoffs to the <a href="/wiki/1989%E2%80%9390_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1989–90 Phoenix Suns season">Suns</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126">[126]</a></sup> Riley announced he was stepping down after the season citing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnout_(psychology)" title="Burnout (psychology)">burnout</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-127"><a href="#cite_note-127">[127]</a></sup> and was replaced by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mike_Dunleavy,_Sr." title="Mike Dunleavy, Sr.">Mike Dunleavy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazenby._p._269_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lazenby._p._269-128">[128]</a></sup> Riley's departure received a mixed reaction from the players. They respected his contributions, but some, such as Worthy and Scott, had grown tired of his intense practices and felt he tried to take too much credit for the team's successes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129">[129]</a></sup> Following the season, 1987 <a href="/wiki/NBA_Defensive_Player_of_the_Year_Award" title="NBA Defensive Player of the Year Award">Defensive Player of the Year</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cooper" title="Michael Cooper">Michael Cooper</a> decided to play in Europe and was waived at his request.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazenby,_p._269_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lazenby,_p._269-130">[130]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/1990%E2%80%9391_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1990–91 Los Angeles Lakers season">1990–91 Lakers</a> failed to win the Pacific Division for the first time in 10 years, but still finished with a 58–24 record. After cruising through the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1991_NBA_Playoffs" title="1991 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference playoffs</a>, the Lakers found themselves in the <a href="/wiki/1991_NBA_Finals" title="1991 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> once again, their ninth trip to the Finals in 12 years. The 1991 Finals represented a changing of the guard as the Lakers were defeated in five games by the <a href="/wiki/1990%E2%80%9391_Chicago_Bulls_season" title="1990–91 Chicago Bulls season">Chicago Bulls</a>, led by superstar <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jordan" title="Michael Jordan">Michael Jordan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131">[131]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>On November 7, 1991, Magic Johnson announced he had tested positive for <a href="/wiki/HIV" title="HIV">HIV</a> and would retire immediately.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132">[132]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In their <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1991–92 Los Angeles Lakers season">first season</a> without Johnson, the team won 43 games to earn the eighth seed in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1992_NBA_Playoffs" title="1992 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference playoffs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-5"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup> The Lakers were defeated in the first round by <a href="/wiki/1991%E2%80%9392_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1991–92 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a>. Following the season, head coach Mike Dunleavy was fired.
</p>
<p>The Lakers would lose 43 games in <a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1992–93 Los Angeles Lakers season">1992–93</a> under <a href="/wiki/Randy_Pfund" title="Randy Pfund">Randy Pfund</a>, their first losing season since 1976. The Lakers would still make the playoffs, and would become the first eighth seed to win the opening two games on the road against a number one seed when they took a 2–0 lead against <a href="/wiki/1992%E2%80%9393_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1992–93 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La3045_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-La3045-133">[133]</a></sup> They lost the next two games at home however, then game five in Phoenix in overtime.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La3045_133-1"><a href="#cite_note-La3045-133">[133]</a></sup> During the <a href="/wiki/1993%E2%80%9394_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1993–94 Los Angeles Lakers season">1993–94 season</a>, Pfund was fired during the season that would result in the Lakers failing to make the playoffs for the first time since 1976. Magic Johnson, would coach the final 16 games of the season with former teammate Michael Cooper as his lead assistant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-134"><a href="#cite_note-134">[134]</a></sup> Johnson decided not to take the job permanently due to what he felt was a lack of commitment from certain players, and Los Angeles ended the season with a 10-game losing streak to finish 33–49.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazenby,_p._307_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lazenby,_p._307-135">[135]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-136"><a href="#cite_note-136">[136]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Under new coach <a href="/wiki/Del_Harris" title="Del Harris">Del Harris</a>, Los Angeles made the playoffs each of the next two seasons, but was eliminated in the second and first rounds respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-137"><a href="#cite_note-137">[137]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-138"><a href="#cite_note-138">[138]</a></sup> The team was led by young guards <a href="/wiki/Nick_Van_Exel" title="Nick Van Exel">Nick Van Exel</a> and <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Jones_(basketball)" title="Eddie Jones (basketball)">Eddie Jones</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lazenby._p._307_139-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lazenby._p._307-139">[139]</a></sup> Johnson came out of retirement to return as a player in the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1995–96 Los Angeles Lakers season">1995–96 season</a> to lead the then 24–18 Lakers to a 29–11 finish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-140"><a href="#cite_note-140">[140]</a></sup> After some run-ins with Van Exel, displeasure with Harris's strategies, and a first round loss to the <a href="/wiki/1995%E2%80%9396_Houston_Rockets_season" title="1995–96 Houston Rockets season">Rockets</a>, Johnson decided to retire for the final time after the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-141"><a href="#cite_note-141">[141]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the 1996 off-season, the Lakers acquired 17-year-old <a href="/wiki/Kobe_Bryant" title="Kobe Bryant">Kobe Bryant</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Hornets" title="Charlotte Hornets">Charlotte Hornets</a> for <a href="/wiki/Vlade_Divac" title="Vlade Divac">Vlade Divac</a>; Bryant was drafted 13th overall out of <a href="/wiki/Lower_Merion_High_School" title="Lower Merion High School">Lower Merion High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Ardmore,_Pennsylvania" title="Ardmore, Pennsylvania">Ardmore, Pennsylvania</a> in <a href="/wiki/1996_NBA_draft" title="1996 NBA draft">that year's draft</a>, by Charlotte. Los Angeles also signed free-agent <a href="/wiki/Shaquille_O%27Neal" title="Shaquille O'Neal">Shaquille O'Neal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-142"><a href="#cite_note-142">[142]</a></sup> Trading for Bryant was West's idea, and he was influential in the team's signing of the all-star center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-143"><a href="#cite_note-143">[143]</a></sup> "Jerry West is the reason I came to the Lakers", O'Neal later said.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-144"><a href="#cite_note-144">[144]</a></sup> They used their 24th pick in the <a href="/wiki/1996_NBA_draft" title="1996 NBA draft">1996 draft</a> to select <a href="/wiki/Derek_Fisher" title="Derek Fisher">Derek Fisher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-145"><a href="#cite_note-145">[145]</a></sup> During <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1996–97 Los Angeles Lakers season">the season</a>, the team traded <a href="/wiki/Cedric_Ceballos" title="Cedric Ceballos">Cedric Ceballos</a> to Phoenix for <a href="/wiki/Robert_Horry" title="Robert Horry">Robert Horry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynasty_No._5:_Los_Angeles_Lakers_146-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dynasty_No._5:_Los_Angeles_Lakers-146">[146]</a></sup> O'Neal led the team to a 56–26 record, their best effort since 1990–91, despite missing 31 games due to a knee injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_96_147-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_96-147">[147]</a></sup> O'Neal averaged 26.2 ppg and 12.5 rpg and finished third in the league in blocked shots (2.88 bpg) in 51 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_96_147-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_96-147">[147]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-148"><a href="#cite_note-148">[148]</a></sup> The Lakers defeated the <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1996–97 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland Trail Blazers</a> in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1997_NBA_Playoffs" title="1997 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a> as O'Neal scored 46 points in Game 1 against the Trail Blazers, marking the highest single-game playoff scoring output by a Laker since Jerry West scored 53 against the Celtics in 1969.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_96_147-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_96-147">[147]</a></sup> In the next round, the Lakers lost in five games to the <a href="/wiki/1996%E2%80%9397_Utah_Jazz_season" title="1996–97 Utah Jazz season">Utah Jazz</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_96_147-3"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_96-147">[147]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1997–98 Los Angeles Lakers season">1997–98 season</a>, O'Neal and the Lakers had the best start in franchise history, 11–0.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup> O'Neal would miss 20 games on the season due to an abdominal injury.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup> Los Angeles battled <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Seattle_SuperSonics_season" title="1997–98 Seattle SuperSonics season">Seattle</a> for the Pacific Division title most of the season. In the final two months, the Lakers won 22 of their final 25 games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-2"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup> finishing 61–21, but still finished second to Seattle in the standings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-3"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup> The Lakers defeated <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1997–98 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a> three games to one in the first round to advance to face Seattle. Although the Sonics won the first game, the Lakers responded with four straight wins, taking the series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-4"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup> but were swept by the <a href="/wiki/1997%E2%80%9398_Utah_Jazz_season" title="1997–98 Utah Jazz season">Jazz</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1998_NBA_Playoffs" title="1998 NBA Playoffs">Western Conference Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33_149-5"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_History_#33-149">[149]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1998–99 Los Angeles Lakers season">1998–99 season</a>, All-Star guard <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Jones_(basketball)" title="Eddie Jones (basketball)">Eddie Jones</a> and center <a href="/wiki/Elden_Campbell" title="Elden Campbell">Elden Campbell</a> were traded to the Charlotte Hornets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-150"><a href="#cite_note-150">[150]</a></sup> The team also acquired <a href="/wiki/J._R._Reid" title="J. R. Reid">J. R. Reid</a>, <a href="/wiki/B._J._Armstrong" title="B. J. Armstrong">B. J. Armstrong</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Glen_Rice" title="Glen Rice">Glen Rice</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-151"><a href="#cite_note-151">[151]</a></sup> Head coach Del Harris was fired in February after a three-game losing streak and replaced on an interim basis by former Laker <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Rambis" title="Kurt Rambis">Kurt Rambis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-152"><a href="#cite_note-152">[152]</a></sup> The team finished 31–19 in the strike-shortened season, which was fourth in the Western Conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-153"><a href="#cite_note-153">[153]</a></sup> The Lakers defeated <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_Houston_Rockets_season" title="1998–99 Houston Rockets season">Houston</a> in the first round of the playoffs, but were swept by <a href="/wiki/1998%E2%80%9399_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="1998–99 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a> in the next round. Game four of the series would be the last game ever played at the <a href="/wiki/The_Forum_(Inglewood,_California)" title="The Forum (Inglewood, California)">Great Western Forum</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-154"><a href="#cite_note-154">[154]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Before the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_NBA_season" title="1999–2000 NBA season">1999–2000 season</a>, West was prepared to hire Rambis as the team's full-time coach before an outcry from fans and members of the organization caused him to seek out a bigger name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-155"><a href="#cite_note-155">[155]</a></sup> Los Angeles hired former <a href="/wiki/Chicago_Bulls" title="Chicago Bulls">Chicago Bulls</a> coach <a href="/wiki/Phil_Jackson" title="Phil Jackson">Phil Jackson</a>, who had coached that team to six championships, and gave him a lucrative $6 million a year contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-156"><a href="#cite_note-156">[156]</a></sup> He brought along assistant <a href="/wiki/Tex_Winter" title="Tex Winter">Tex Winter</a> and they installed Winter's version of the <a href="/wiki/Triangle_offense" title="Triangle offense">triangle offense</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-157"><a href="#cite_note-157">[157]</a></sup> The Lakers signed veterans <a href="/wiki/Brian_Shaw" title="Brian Shaw">Brian Shaw</a>, <a href="/wiki/John_Salley" title="John Salley">John Salley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ron_Harper" title="Ron Harper">Ron Harper</a>, and <a href="/wiki/A._C._Green" title="A. C. Green">A. C. Green</a>, who was a Laker during the "Showtime" era.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynasty_No._5:_Los_Angeles_Lakers_146-1"><a href="#cite_note-Dynasty_No._5:_Los_Angeles_Lakers-146">[146]</a></sup> The team also moved to a new arena, the Staples Center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#35_158-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#35-158">[158]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Led by league MVP O'Neal, the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1999–2000 Los Angeles Lakers season">Lakers</a> won 31 of their first 36 games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_1999–2000_Summary_159-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_1999–2000_Summary-159">[159]</a></sup> They finished 67–15, the highest win total since they won 65 in the <a href="/wiki/1986%E2%80%9387_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1986–87 Los Angeles Lakers season">1986–87 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_1999–2000_Summary_159-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_1999–2000_Summary-159">[159]</a></sup> The eliminated <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Sacramento_Kings_season" title="1999–2000 Sacramento Kings season">Sacramento</a> and <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="1999–2000 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix</a> in the first two rounds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2000_NBA_Playoffs" title="2000 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-160"><a href="#cite_note-160">[160]</a></sup> After the Lakers took a three games to one lead in the Western Conference Finals against <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="1999–2000 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a>, the Trail Blazers won the next two games to force a game seven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blazers'_dry_spell_allows_Lakers_to_take_Game_7_161-0"><a href="#cite_note-Blazers'_dry_spell_allows_Lakers_to_take_Game_7-161">[161]</a></sup> The Lakers, who trailed by 15 points in the fourth quarter, would go on 19–4 run to tie the game and eventually win 89–84 to advance to the <a href="/wiki/2000_NBA_Finals" title="2000 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blazers'_dry_spell_allows_Lakers_to_take_Game_7_161-1"><a href="#cite_note-Blazers'_dry_spell_allows_Lakers_to_take_Game_7-161">[161]</a></sup> In their first trip to the Finals since <a href="/wiki/1991_NBA_Finals" title="1991 NBA Finals">1991</a>, the Lakers defeated <a href="/wiki/Reggie_Miller" title="Reggie Miller">Reggie Miller</a> and the <a href="/wiki/1999%E2%80%932000_Indiana_Pacers_season" title="1999–2000 Indiana Pacers season">Indiana Pacers</a> four games to two win their first title since <a href="/wiki/1987%E2%80%9388_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1987–88 Los Angeles Lakers season">1988</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-162"><a href="#cite_note-162">[162]</a></sup> West retired from his spot in the team's front office after the season after a power struggle between him and Jackson over control of the team's operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-163"><a href="#cite_note-163">[163]</a></sup> After the season, starters Rice and Green left the team,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-164"><a href="#cite_note-164">[164]</a></sup> and Los Angeles signed <a href="/wiki/Horace_Grant" title="Horace Grant">Horace Grant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-165"><a href="#cite_note-165">[165]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2000–01 Los Angeles Lakers season">following season</a>, the Lakers won 11 fewer regular season games than the prior year,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-166"><a href="#cite_note-166">[166]</a></sup> but swept the first three rounds of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2001_NBA_Playoffs" title="2001 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a>, defeating the <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="2000–01 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a>, <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_Sacramento_Kings_season" title="2000–01 Sacramento Kings season">Sacramento</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="2000–01 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-167"><a href="#cite_note-167">[167]</a></sup> They met <a href="/wiki/Allen_Iverson" title="Allen Iverson">Allen Iverson</a> and the <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_Philadelphia_76ers_season" title="2000–01 Philadelphia 76ers season">Philadelphia 76ers</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2001_NBA_Finals" title="2001 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>. Although the Sixers took game one in overtime,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-168"><a href="#cite_note-168">[168]</a></sup> the Lakers won the next four games to win their second straight title. Their 15–1 postseason record was the best in NBA history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-169"><a href="#cite_note-169">[169]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers won 58 games in <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_NBA_season" title="2001–02 NBA season">2001–02</a>. In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2002_NBA_Playoffs" title="2002 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a>, they swept <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Portland_Trail_Blazers_season" title="2001–02 Portland Trail Blazers season">Portland</a> and defeated <a href="/wiki/2000%E2%80%9301_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="2000–01 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a> four games to one to advance to the Western Conference Finals to face <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_Sacramento_Kings_season" title="2001–02 Sacramento Kings season">Sacramento</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2002_ballref_172-0"><a href="#cite_note-2002_ballref-172">[172]</a></sup> The series would go on to be known as one of the greatest playoff matchups in NBA history. The series extended to all seven games, and ended in a Lakers victory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-173"><a href="#cite_note-173">[173]</a></sup> In game one, Bryant scored 30 points as the Lakers won, 106–99. The series would then shift in Sacramento's favor, with the Kings winning the next two games. Facing a deficit in game 4, the Lakers had the ball with under 20 seconds to play. After misses by both Bryant and O'Neal, Kings center <a href="/wiki/Vlade_Divac" title="Vlade Divac">Vlade Divac</a> tapped the ball away from the rim in an attempt to wind down the clock. It went straight into <a href="/wiki/Robert_Horry" title="Robert Horry">Robert Horry</a>'s hands, who drained a game-winning three with under three seconds to play. After the Kings won game five on a buzzer beater by <a href="/wiki/Mike_Bibby" title="Mike Bibby">Mike Bibby</a>, the Lakers were faced with a must-win game six. In one of the most controversial playoff games in league history (<a href="/wiki/Tim_Donaghy" title="Tim Donaghy">Tim Donaghy scandal</a>), the Lakers would win by four points.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-174"><a href="#cite_note-174">[174]</a></sup> The Lakers went on to win game seven in overtime, with the Kings missing numerous potentially game-saving shots and free throws. The Lakers then achieved a three-peat by sweeping <a href="/wiki/Jason_Kidd" title="Jason Kidd">Jason Kidd</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Jersey_Nets" title="New Jersey Nets">New Jersey Nets</a> in the <a href="/wiki/2002_NBA_Finals" title="2002 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-175"><a href="#cite_note-175">[175]</a></sup> O'Neal won each of the Finals series' MVP awards, making him the only player besides <a href="/wiki/Michael_Jordan" title="Michael Jordan">Michael Jordan</a> to win three consecutive Finals MVPs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-finalsmvp_176-0"><a href="#cite_note-finalsmvp-176">[176]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2002–03 Los Angeles Lakers season">Lakers</a> would attempt a four-peat the following year, but started the <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_NBA_season" title="2002–03 NBA season">2002–03 season</a> 11–19.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-177"><a href="#cite_note-177">[177]</a></sup> However, they finished the season 39–13 to finish 50–32.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2002–03_Summary_178-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2002–03_Summary-178">[178]</a></sup> They defeated <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_Minnesota_Timberwolves_season" title="2002–03 Minnesota Timberwolves season">Minnesota</a> in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2003_NBA_Playoffs" title="2003 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a>, but the four-peat attempt ended as they were eliminated by <a href="/wiki/2002%E2%80%9303_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="2002–03 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a> in six games in the second round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-179"><a href="#cite_note-179">[179]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2003–04 Los Angeles Lakers season">2003–04 season</a>, the team was the subject of intense media coverage generated by the teaming of four stars and the <a href="/wiki/Kobe_Bryant_sexual_assault_case" title="Kobe Bryant sexual assault case">sexual-assault case involving Kobe Bryant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-180"><a href="#cite_note-180">[180]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-181"><a href="#cite_note-181">[181]</a></sup> Before the season, the Lakers signed two-time MVP <a href="/wiki/Karl_Malone" title="Karl Malone">Karl Malone</a> formerly of the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Jazz</a>, and former <a href="/wiki/Seattle_SuperSonics" title="Seattle SuperSonics">Seattle</a> Defensive Player of the Year <a href="/wiki/Gary_Payton" title="Gary Payton">Gary Payton</a> to join O'Neal and Bryant.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-182"><a href="#cite_note-182">[182]</a></sup> Three of the "big four", however, struggled with injuries: O'Neal suffered from a strained calf, Malone an injured knee, and Bryant an injured shoulder.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-183"><a href="#cite_note-183">[183]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-184"><a href="#cite_note-184">[184]</a></sup> The Lakers started 18–3 and finished 56–26 and won the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Division_(NBA)" title="Pacific Division (NBA)">Pacific Division</a> title, entering the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_NBA_Playoffs" title="2004 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a> as the No. 2 seed. They defeated <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Houston_Rockets_season" title="2003–04 Houston Rockets season">Houston</a>, <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_San_Antonio_Spurs_season" title="2003–04 San Antonio Spurs season">San Antonio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Minnesota_Timberwolves_season" title="2003–04 Minnesota Timberwolves season">Minnesota</a> to advance to the <a href="/wiki/2004_NBA_Finals" title="2004 NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-185"><a href="#cite_note-185">[185]</a></sup> In the Finals, they would lose to <a href="/wiki/2003%E2%80%9304_Detroit_Pistons_season" title="2003–04 Detroit Pistons season">Detroit</a> in five games.
</p>
<p>During the 2004 off-season, the team entered a rebuilding phase when O'Neal was traded to the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Heat" title="Miami Heat">Miami Heat</a> for <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Odom" title="Lamar Odom">Lamar Odom</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_Grant" title="Brian Grant">Brian Grant</a>, <a href="/wiki/Caron_Butler" title="Caron Butler">Caron Butler</a>, and a first-round draft pick.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-186"><a href="#cite_note-186">[186]</a></sup> Bryant and O'Neal had <a href="/wiki/Shaq%E2%80%93Kobe_feud" title="Shaq–Kobe feud">clashed in the past</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-187"><a href="#cite_note-187">[187]</a></sup> and the media credited their feud as one of the motivating factors for the trade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-188"><a href="#cite_note-188">[188]</a></sup> Jackson did not return as head coach, and <a href="/wiki/The_Last_Season:_A_Team_in_Search_of_Its_Soul" title="The Last Season: A Team in Search of Its Soul">wrote a book</a> about the team's 2003–04 season, in which he heavily criticized Bryant and called him "uncoachable".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-189"><a href="#cite_note-189">[189]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-190"><a href="#cite_note-190">[190]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-191"><a href="#cite_note-191">[191]</a></sup> The Lakers front office said that the book contained "several inaccuracies".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-192"><a href="#cite_note-192">[192]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers also traded <a href="/wiki/Rick_Fox" title="Rick Fox">Rick Fox</a> and Gary Payton to Boston, for <a href="/wiki/Chris_Mihm" title="Chris Mihm">Chris Mihm</a>, <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Banks" title="Marcus Banks">Marcus Banks</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Chucky_Atkins" title="Chucky Atkins">Chucky Atkins</a> before the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_NBA_season" title="2004–05 NBA season">2004–05 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal_193-0"><a href="#cite_note-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal-193">[193]</a></sup> Derek Fisher, frustrated with losing playing time, opted out of his contract and signed with the Warriors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal_193-1"><a href="#cite_note-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal-193">[193]</a></sup> The team hired <a href="/wiki/Rudy_Tomjanovich" title="Rudy Tomjanovich">Rudy Tomjanovich</a> to replace Jackson.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal_193-2"><a href="#cite_note-L.A._gets_Mihm,_Banks,_Atkins_in_deal-193">[193]</a></sup> After sitting out the first half of the <a href="/wiki/2004%E2%80%9305_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2004–05 Los Angeles Lakers season">2004–05 season</a>, Malone announced his retirement on February 13, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-194"><a href="#cite_note-194">[194]</a></sup> Tomjanovich coached the team to a 22–19 record before resigning due to health problems.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-195"><a href="#cite_note-195">[195]</a></sup> Assistant <a href="/wiki/Frank_Hamblen" title="Frank Hamblen">Frank Hamblen</a> was named interim head coach to replace Tomjanovich for the remainder of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-196"><a href="#cite_note-196">[196]</a></sup> Bryant (ankle) and Odom (shoulder) suffered injuries, and the Lakers finished 34–48, missing the playoffs for only the fifth time in franchise history and the first time since <a href="/wiki/1993%E2%80%9394_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="1993–94 Los Angeles Lakers season">1994</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-6"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>With the 10th overall pick in the <a href="/wiki/2005_NBA_draft" title="2005 NBA draft">2005 draft</a>, the Lakers selected <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bynum" title="Andrew Bynum">Andrew Bynum</a>, a center from <a href="/wiki/St._Joseph_High_School_(Metuchen,_New_Jersey)" title="St. Joseph High School (Metuchen, New Jersey)">St. Joseph High School</a> in <a href="/wiki/Metuchen,_New_Jersey" title="Metuchen, New Jersey">Metuchen, New Jersey</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-197"><a href="#cite_note-197">[197]</a></sup> The team also traded Caron Butler and Chucky Atkins to <a href="/wiki/Washington_Wizards" title="Washington Wizards">Washington</a> for <a href="/wiki/Kwame_Brown" title="Kwame Brown">Kwame Brown</a> and <a href="/wiki/Laron_Profit" title="Laron Profit">Laron Profit</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-198"><a href="#cite_note-198">[198]</a></sup> Jackson returned to coach the <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2005–06 Los Angeles Lakers season">team</a> after Rudy Tomjanovich resigned midway through the previous season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-199"><a href="#cite_note-199">[199]</a></sup> On January 22, 2006, Bryant scored 81 points against <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Toronto_Raptors_season" title="2005–06 Toronto Raptors season">Toronto</a>, the second-highest total in NBA history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-200"><a href="#cite_note-200">[200]</a></sup> Ending the season 45–37, the team made the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2005_NBA_Playoffs" title="2005 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a> after a one-season absence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-201"><a href="#cite_note-201">[201]</a></sup> After taking a three games to one lead in the first round, <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="2005–06 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix</a> came back to take the series in seven games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-202"><a href="#cite_note-202">[202]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2005–06 Los Angeles Lakers season">the following season</a>, the Lakers won 26 of their first 39 games,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary_203-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary-203">[203]</a></sup> but lost 27 of their last 43—including seven in a row at one point—to finish 42–40.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary_203-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary-203">[203]</a></sup> They were eliminated in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2006_NBA_Playoffs" title="2006 NBA Playoffs">first round</a> by <a href="/wiki/2005%E2%80%9306_Phoenix_Suns_season" title="2005–06 Phoenix Suns season">Phoenix</a> again.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary_203-2"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_2006–07_Summary-203">[203]</a></sup> Frustrated by the team's inability to advance in the playoffs, Bryant demanded to be traded in the off-season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-204"><a href="#cite_note-204">[204]</a></sup> Buss initially agreed to seek a trade,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-205"><a href="#cite_note-205">[205]</a></sup> but also worked to try to change Bryant's mind.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-206"><a href="#cite_note-206">[206]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>After re-acquiring <a href="/wiki/Derek_Fisher" title="Derek Fisher">Derek Fisher</a>, the Lakers started the <a href="/wiki/2007%E2%80%9308_NBA_season" title="2007–08 NBA season">2007–08 season</a> with a 25–11 record, before <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Bynum" title="Andrew Bynum">Andrew Bynum</a>, their center who was leading the league in field-goal percentage, went out for the year due to a knee injury in mid-January.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-207"><a href="#cite_note-207">[207]</a></sup> In what would become a crucial transfer for the franchise's return to championship form, they acquired the six-time all-star power forward <a href="/wiki/Pau_Gasol" title="Pau Gasol">Pau Gasol</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Memphis_Grizzlies" title="Memphis Grizzlies">Memphis Grizzlies</a> in a trade in early February and went 22–5 to finish the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-208"><a href="#cite_note-208">[208]</a></sup> The Lakers' 57–25 record earned them the first seed in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Conference_(NBA)" title="Western Conference (NBA)">Western Conference</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-209"><a href="#cite_note-209">[209]</a></sup> Bryant was awarded the league's MVP award, becoming the first Laker to win the award since O'Neal in 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-210"><a href="#cite_note-210">[210]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-211"><a href="#cite_note-211">[211]</a></sup> In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2008_NBA_Playoffs" title="2008 NBA Playoffs">the playoffs</a>, they defeated the Nuggets in four games, the Jazz in six, and the defending champion Spurs in five, but lost to the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Celtics</a> in six games in <a href="/wiki/2008_NBA_Finals" title="2008 NBA Finals">the NBA Finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-212"><a href="#cite_note-212">[212]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NBA_season" title="2008–09 NBA season">2008–09 season</a>, the Lakers finished 65–17; the best record in the Western Conference.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-213"><a href="#cite_note-213">[213]</a></sup> They defeated the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Jazz</a> in five games, the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Rockets" title="Houston Rockets">Rockets</a> in seven and the <a href="/wiki/Denver_Nuggets" title="Denver Nuggets">Nuggets</a> in six, to win the Western Conference title. They then won their 15th NBA championship by defeating the <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Magic" title="Orlando Magic">Orlando Magic</a> in five games in <a href="/wiki/2009_NBA_Finals" title="2009 NBA Finals">the NBA finals</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-214"><a href="#cite_note-214">[214]</a></sup> Bryant was named the NBA Finals MVP for the first time in his career.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-215"><a href="#cite_note-215">[215]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers, who had added <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ron_Artest" title="Ron Artest">Ron Artest</a> (now Metta World Peace)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-216"><a href="#cite_note-216">[216]</a></sup> in place of <a href="/wiki/Trevor_Ariza" title="Trevor Ariza">Trevor Ariza</a> in their starting lineup, finished <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_NBA_season" title="2009–10 NBA season">the 2009–10 season</a> with the best record in the <a href="/wiki/Western_Conference_(NBA)" title="Western Conference (NBA)">Western Conference</a> for the third straight time. On January 13, 2010, the Lakers became the first team in NBA history to win 3,000 regular season games by defeating the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Mavericks" title="Dallas Mavericks">Dallas Mavericks</a> 100–95.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-217"><a href="#cite_note-217">[217]</a></sup> They defeated the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_Thunder" title="Oklahoma City Thunder">Oklahoma City Thunder</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Utah_Jazz" title="Utah Jazz">Utah Jazz</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Suns" title="Phoenix Suns">Phoenix Suns</a> in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Playoffs" title="NBA Playoffs">Western Conference playoffs</a>. In <a href="/wiki/2010_NBA_Finals" title="2010 NBA Finals">the finals</a>, the Lakers played the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Boston Celtics</a> for the 12th time. They rallied back from a 3–2 disadvantage in the series and erased a 13-point deficit in the fourth quarter of the seventh game to defeat the Celtics. This series win gave them their 16th NBA title overall and 11th since they moved to Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-218"><a href="#cite_note-218">[218]</a></sup> Bryant was named Finals MVP for the second year in a row, despite a 6–24 shooting performance in game seven.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-219"><a href="#cite_note-219">[219]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>After much speculation, head coach Phil Jackson returned for the <a href="/wiki/2010%E2%80%9311_NBA_season" title="2010–11 NBA season">2010–11 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-220"><a href="#cite_note-220">[220]</a></sup> In the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2011_NBA_Playoffs" title="2011 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a>, the Lakers defeated the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Orleans_Hornets" title="New Orleans Hornets">New Orleans Hornets</a> in the first round.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-221"><a href="#cite_note-221">[221]</a></sup> But their opportunity for a three-peat was denied by the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Mavericks" title="Dallas Mavericks">Dallas Mavericks</a> in a four-game sweep of the second round. After the season, it was announced that Jackson will not be returning to coach the Lakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-222"><a href="#cite_note-222">[222]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>After Jackson's retirement, former <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Cavaliers" title="Cleveland Cavaliers">Cleveland Cavaliers</a> head coach <a href="/wiki/Mike_Brown_(basketball,_born_1970)" title="Mike Brown (basketball, born 1970)">Mike Brown</a> was hired as head coach on May 25, 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-223"><a href="#cite_note-223">[223]</a></sup> Before the start of the <a href="/wiki/2011_NBA_lockout" title="2011 NBA lockout">shortened</a> <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_NBA_season" title="2011–12 NBA season">2011–12</a> season, the Lakers traded <a href="/wiki/Lamar_Odom" title="Lamar Odom">Lamar Odom</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Dallas_Mavericks" title="Dallas Mavericks">Dallas Mavericks</a> after Odom requested to be traded.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-224"><a href="#cite_note-224">[224]</a></sup> On the trade deadline long time Laker <a href="/wiki/Derek_Fisher" title="Derek Fisher">Derek Fisher</a> along with a first round draft pick were traded to the <a href="/wiki/Houston_Rockets" title="Houston Rockets">Houston Rockets</a> for <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Hill_(basketball)" title="Jordan Hill (basketball)">Jordan Hill</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-225"><a href="#cite_note-225">[225]</a></sup> With a 41–25 regular season record the Lakers entered the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2012_NBA_Playoffs" title="2012 NBA Playoffs">playoffs</a> as the third seed, the team defeated the <a href="/wiki/Denver_Nuggets" title="Denver Nuggets">Denver Nuggets</a> in the first round in seven games but were eliminated by the <a href="/wiki/Oklahoma_City_Thunder" title="Oklahoma City Thunder">Oklahoma City Thunder</a> in the second round in five games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-226"><a href="#cite_note-226">[226]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-227"><a href="#cite_note-227">[227]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>On July 4, 2012, <a href="/wiki/Steve_Nash" title="Steve Nash">Steve Nash</a> of the Phoenix Suns agreed to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign_and_trade" title="Sign and trade">sign-and-trade</a> deal that would send him to the Lakers in exchange for the Lakers' 2013 and 2015 first round draft picks, 2013 and 2014 second round draft picks, and $3 million. The trade was made official on July 11, 2012, the first day the trade moratorium was lifted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-228"><a href="#cite_note-228">[228]</a></sup> On August 10, 2012, in a four-team trade the Lakers traded Andrew Bynum and acquired <a href="/wiki/Dwight_Howard" title="Dwight Howard">Dwight Howard</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-229"><a href="#cite_note-229">[229]</a></sup> On November 9, 2012, Mike Brown was relieved of coaching duties after a 1–4 start to the <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2012–13 Los Angeles Lakers season">2012–13</a> season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-230"><a href="#cite_note-230">[230]</a></sup> Assistant Coach Bernie Bickerstaff took over as interim head coach, leading the Lakers to a 5–5 record. On November 12, 2012, the Lakers hired <a href="/wiki/Mike_D%27Antoni" title="Mike D'Antoni">Mike D'Antoni</a> as head coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-231"><a href="#cite_note-231">[231]</a></sup> On February 18, 2013, Lakers owner Jerry Buss died from cancer at age 80.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-232"><a href="#cite_note-232">[232]</a></sup> On the court, D'Antoni coached the Lakers to a 40–32 record the rest of the way to finish 45–37, their worst record since <a href="/wiki/2006%E2%80%9307_NBA_season" title="2006–07 NBA season">2007</a>. The Lakers clinched a playoff berth on the final game of the season and finished seventh in the Western Conference after beating the Houston Rockets on April 16, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-233"><a href="#cite_note-233">[233]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-234"><a href="#cite_note-234">[234]</a></sup> The Lakers battled injuries all season, the most prominent of which is the <a href="/wiki/Achilles_tendon_rupture" title="Achilles tendon rupture">Achilles tendon rupture</a> to Kobe Bryant that ended his season after 78 games. The absence of Bryant was sorely felt as the Lakers were swept by the San Antonio Spurs in the first round of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_NBA_Playoffs" title="2013 NBA Playoffs">2013 NBA Playoffs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-235"><a href="#cite_note-235">[235]</a></sup> Nevertheless, Bryant passed Lakers legend Wilt Chamberlain to become the fourth all-time leading scorer in NBA history on March 30, 2013, against the Sacramento Kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-236"><a href="#cite_note-236">[236]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>On December 8, 2013, Bryant played in his first game since tearing his Achilles tendon on April 12, 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-237"><a href="#cite_note-237">[237]</a></sup> However, on December 17, 2013, he suffered a broken bone in his knee, and did not return for the remainder of the season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-238"><a href="#cite_note-238">[238]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-239"><a href="#cite_note-239">[239]</a></sup> On March 25, 2014, the Lakers scored 51 points in the third quarter against the New York Knicks, the most points scored in a quarter in the history of the franchise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-240"><a href="#cite_note-240">[240]</a></sup> The Lakers went on to miss the NBA playoffs for the first time since 2005, for just the second time in the last two decades and for just the sixth time in franchise history. On April 30, 2014, Mike D'Antoni resigned from his position as head coach after a 27–55 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-241"><a href="#cite_note-241">[241]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>After spending the majority of the off-season without a head coach, the Lakers named former player <a href="/wiki/Byron_Scott" title="Byron Scott">Byron Scott</a> as the new head coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-242"><a href="#cite_note-242">[242]</a></sup> After the season, he was the frontrunner to become the new Lakers head coach. Scott interviewed three times for the position, which had become vacant after <a href="/wiki/Mike_D%27Antoni" title="Mike D'Antoni">Mike D'Antoni</a>'s resignation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-243"><a href="#cite_note-243">[243]</a></sup> On July 28, 2014, he signed a multi-year contract to coach the Lakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-244"><a href="#cite_note-244">[244]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>During the first game of the <a href="/wiki/2014%E2%80%9315_Los_Angeles_Lakers_season" title="2014–15 Los Angeles Lakers season">2014–15</a> season, the 7th overall pick in the <a href="/wiki/2014_NBA_draft" title="2014 NBA draft">2014 NBA draft</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julius_Randle" title="Julius Randle">Julius Randle</a> went down with a broken leg, which ended his rookie season. The Lakers began their season losing 10 of their first 16 games. After playing only 35 games, Kobe Bryant tore a rotator cuff in his shoulder ending his season. <a href="/wiki/Nick_Young_(basketball)" title="Nick Young (basketball)">Nick Young</a> was also forced to end his season with a fractured kneecap, leaving the team with a record of 14–41.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-245"><a href="#cite_note-245">[245]</a></sup> With 27 games left in the regular season, Byron Scott gave rookie <a href="/wiki/Jordan_Clarkson" title="Jordan Clarkson">Jordan Clarkson</a> more playing time. Clarkson, the 46th overall pick in the 2014 draft, finished his rookie season with game stats of 11.9 ppg, 3.2 rpg, 3.5 apg, and shooting 44.8% from the field.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-246"><a href="#cite_note-246">[246]</a></sup> The Lakers' season ended with a record of 21–61, the 4th worst record in the league and at the time the worst record in franchise history.
</p>

<p>The rivalry between the <a href="/wiki/Boston_Celtics" title="Boston Celtics">Boston Celtics</a> and the Lakers involves the two most storied <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">basketball</a> franchises in <a href="/wiki/National_Basketball_Association" title="National Basketball Association">National Basketball Association</a> (NBA) history. It has been called the best rivalry in the NBA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-273"><a href="#cite_note-273">[273]</a></sup> The two teams have met a record 12 times in the <a href="/wiki/NBA_Finals" title="NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a>, starting with their first Finals meeting in <a href="/wiki/1959_NBA_Finals" title="1959 NBA Finals">1959</a>. They would go on to dominate the league in the 1960s and the 1980s, facing each other six times in the 1960s and three times in the 1980s.
</p>
<p>The rivalry had been less intense since the retirements of <a href="/wiki/Magic_Johnson" title="Magic Johnson">Magic Johnson</a> and <a href="/wiki/Larry_Bird" title="Larry Bird">Larry Bird</a> in the early 1990s, but in 2008 it was renewed as the Celtics and Lakers met in the Finals for the first time since 1987, with the Celtics winning the series 4–2. They faced off once again in the <a href="/wiki/2010_NBA_Finals" title="2010 NBA Finals">2010 NBA Finals</a>, which the Lakers won in 7 games.
</p>
<p>The two teams have won the two highest numbers of championships, the Celtics 17, the Lakers 17; together, the 34 championships account for almost half of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_NBA_champions" title="List of NBA champions">73 championships</a> in NBA history.
</p>
<p>The all-time series record for the Lakers vs Celtics is 204–162 with the Celtics being the forerunners.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-274"><a href="#cite_note-274">[274]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The rivalry between the Lakers and the <a href="/wiki/Detroit_Pistons" title="Detroit Pistons">Detroit Pistons</a> developed in the late 1980s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-275"><a href="#cite_note-275">[275]</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>228</span></sup> Both teams faced each other in back-to-back finals appearances in the <a href="/wiki/1988_NBA_Finals" title="1988 NBA Finals">1988 NBA Finals</a>, which the Lakers won in 7 games and the <a href="/wiki/1989_NBA_Finals" title="1989 NBA Finals">1989 NBA Finals</a>, which the Pistons won in 4 games.
</p>
<p>The rivalry reemerged in the early 2000s as both teams squared off against one another in the <a href="/wiki/2004_NBA_Finals" title="2004 NBA Finals">2004 NBA Finals</a>, which the Pistons won in five games.
</p>
<p>The rivalry between the Lakers and the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Clippers" title="Los Angeles Clippers">Los Angeles Clippers</a> is unique because they are the only two NBA teams to share an arena, the <a href="/wiki/Staples_Center" title="Staples Center">Staples Center</a>. It is also one of only two intra-city rivalries in the NBA, the other being the new crosstown rivalry between the <a href="/wiki/New_York_Knicks" title="New York Knicks">New York Knicks</a> and <a href="/wiki/Brooklyn_Nets" title="Brooklyn Nets">Brooklyn Nets</a>.
</p>
<p>Los Angeles fans have historically favored the Lakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gold_276-0"><a href="#cite_note-gold-276">[276]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-277"><a href="#cite_note-277">[277]</a></sup> Some contend that the term <i>rivalry</i> is inaccurate until the Clippers become more successful.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-278"><a href="#cite_note-278">[278]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Spurs" title="San Antonio Spurs">San Antonio Spurs</a> and the Lakers, developed what some would classify as a rivalry in the late 1990s and early 2000s. Since 1999, the teams have met in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Playoffs" title="NBA Playoffs">NBA Playoffs</a> five times, with the clubs combining to appear in seven consecutive <a href="/wiki/NBA_Finals" title="NBA Finals">NBA Finals</a> (from 1999 to 2005). Additionally, the teams combined to win five NBA championships from 1999 to 2003; the Spurs won the NBA championship in 1999, 2003, 2005, 2007 and 2014 while the Lakers won the championship in 2000, 2001, 2002, 2009 and 2010. From 1999 to 2004 the clubs' rivalry was often considered the premier rivalry in the NBA,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-279"><a href="#cite_note-279">[279]</a></sup> and each time the clubs faced each other in the playoffs the winner advanced to the NBA Finals. In 2008, the teams met again in the Western Conference Finals where the Spurs were handily defeated only to beat the Lakers when they met again in 2013.
</p>
<p>Berger and Chalfen purchased the NBL's disbanded Detroit Gems for $15,000 in 1947, changed their name to the Lakers and relocated them to Minnesota. Max Winter bought a third of the club in their early years, and sold his share to Mikan in 1954. Berger bought Mikan's share in 1956 giving him a controlling (⅔) interest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirt_280-0"><a href="#cite_note-dirt-280">[280]</a></sup> After Mikan retired, attendance plummeted and the team lost money for several seasons, leading the ownership group to put the team up for sale in 1957.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sara_281-0"><a href="#cite_note-Sara-281">[281]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marty_Marion" title="Marty Marion">Marty Marion</a>, a retired baseball player and manager, and his business partner Milton Fischman attempted to purchase the team with the intention of moving the club to <a href="/wiki/Kansas_City,_Missouri" title="Kansas City, Missouri">Kansas City, Missouri</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sara_281-1"><a href="#cite_note-Sara-281">[281]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minn_282-0"><a href="#cite_note-Minn-282">[282]</a></sup> Mikan offered to mortgage his home in an attempt to buy the team and keep the club in Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-283"><a href="#cite_note-283">[283]</a></sup> The Lakers were sold to a group of investors led by Bob Short however.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirt_280-1"><a href="#cite_note-dirt-280">[280]</a></sup> The team was sold to Short's group with the agreement that it would not be relocated to Kansas City but kept in Minnesota.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mil_284-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mil-284">[284]</a></sup> Short's ownership group consisted of 117 Minnesota businesses and private citizens, who amassed a total of $200,000 for the purchase; $150,000 to buy the team and $50,000 to run it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirt_280-2"><a href="#cite_note-dirt-280">[280]</a></sup> By 1958 Short had become 80% owner of the team by buying out his partners,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dirt_280-3"><a href="#cite_note-dirt-280">[280]</a></sup> but the team was floundering. Attendance remained poor, and the NBA had put the Lakers on "financial probation", notifying them that if they did not meet certain ticket sales numbers they could be bought out by the league and relocated. Short was forced to move the team to Los Angeles in 1960; the club had lost $60,000 in the first half of the 1959–60 season alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-285"><a href="#cite_note-285">[285]</a></sup> The NBA's owners originally voted 7–1 against the move.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-286"><a href="#cite_note-286">[286]</a></sup> When Short indicated that he might take the team to new rival league that was developing however, the owners held another vote that same day and allowed the relocation (8–0).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-287"><a href="#cite_note-287">[287]</a></sup> Aided by Baylor's drawing power,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-288"><a href="#cite_note-288">[288]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-289"><a href="#cite_note-289">[289]</a></sup> and the new locale, the team's finances improved when they arrived in LA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-290"><a href="#cite_note-290">[290]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-291"><a href="#cite_note-291">[291]</a></sup> Short sold the team to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_Redskins" title="Washington Redskins">Washington Redskins</a> owner and publisher Jack Kent Cooke in 1965 for a then league record amount of $5.175 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-292"><a href="#cite_note-292">[292]</a></sup> Short insisted the deal be conducted in cash as he was wary of Cooke, so guards transported the money in a cart from one New York bank to another.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-293"><a href="#cite_note-293">[293]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>Cooke was a more hands-on owner than Short, and overhauled the team's operations.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-294"><a href="#cite_note-294">[294]</a></sup> He personally financed construction of the Forum in 1967 at a cost of $16.5 million.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-295"><a href="#cite_note-295">[295]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Star_296-0"><a href="#cite_note-FB2009-301">[301]</a></sup> and until October 2010 Magic Johnson was a minority owner as well.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-302"><a href="#cite_note-302">[302]</a></sup> Buss (1919–97)">Great Western</a> Forum in 1988.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-303"><a href="#cite_note-303">[303]</a></sup> In 2009 major sponsors included <a href="/wiki/Verizon_Wireless" title="Verizon Wireless">Verizon Wireless</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Anheuser-Busch" title="Anheuser-Busch">Anheuser-Busch</a>, <a Baylor, Bryant and West hold the top five single season scoring averages, with Bryant occupying the numbers one (35.4) and four (31.6) spots, while Baylor has the second (34.8), and third (34.0), and West the fifth (31.3).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-319"><a href="#cite_note-319">[319]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-320"><a href="#cite_note-320">[320]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>The Lakers hold several NBA records as a team including most consecutive games won overall (33) and most consecutive road games won (16) both of which came during the 1971–72 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teamrec_321-0"><a href="#cite_note-teamrec-321">[321]</a></sup> Highest field-goal percentage for a season at 54.5% (1984–85),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-322"><a href="#cite_note-322">[322]</a></sup> and highest road winning percentage at 0.816 (1971–72).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nba10_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-nba10-58">[58]</a></sup> They also hold records for having (into the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_NBA_season" title="2009–10 NBA season">2009–10 season</a>) the most wins (3,027), the highest winning percentage (61.9%), and the most NBA Finals appearances (31).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LAL_29-9"><a href="#cite_note-LAL-29">[29]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-team_323-0"><a href="#cite_note-team-323">[323]</a></sup> The 2000–01 team set the NBA record for best playoff record at 15–1, which was later broken by the <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> in 2017.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2017PlayoffsRecord_324-0"><a href="#cite_note-2017PlayoffsRecord-324">[324]</a></sup> The 1971–72 team holds franchise records in wins (69), most points scored, and largest margin of victory; both of the latter came in the team's 63 point win versus Golden State (162–99).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-325"><a href="#cite_note-325">[325]</a></sup> They also used to hold the record for most wins at home in the regular season (going 36–5 in 1971–72, then 37–4 in both 1976–77 and 1979–80) before the Boston Celtics set the current record of 40–1 in the 1985–86 season.
</p>
<p>The Lakers play their home games at <a href="/wiki/Staples_Center" title="Staples Center">Staples Center</a>, located at <a href="/wiki/L.A._Live" title="L.A. Live">L.A. Live</a> in <a href="/wiki/Downtown_Los_Angeles" title="Downtown Los Angeles">Downtown Los Angeles</a>. The arena opened in fall 1999, and seats up to 18,997 for Lakers games.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_STAPLES_Center_326-0"><a href="#cite_note-About_STAPLES_Center-326">[326]</a></sup> Owned and operated by <a href="/wiki/Anschutz_Entertainment_Group" title="Anschutz Entertainment Group">AEG</a> and L.A. Arena Company, the arena is also home to the Los Angeles Clippers, the WNBA's Los Angeles Sparks, and the NHL's Los Angeles Kings.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-About_STAPLES_Center_326-1"><a href="#cite_note-About_STAPLES_Center-326">[326]</a></sup> Before moving to Staples Center, for 32 seasons (1967–1999), the Lakers played their home games at <a href="/wiki/The_Forum_(Inglewood,_California)" title="The Forum (Inglewood, California)">The Forum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Inglewood,_California" title="Inglewood, California">Inglewood, California</a>, located approximately 10 miles southwest of the team's current home.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_#35_158-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_#35-158">[158]</a></sup> During the 1999 preseason, the Lakers played their home games at the Forum before officially moving into Staples Center, and once again hosted a preseason game versus the <a href="/wiki/Golden_State_Warriors" title="Golden State Warriors">Golden State Warriors</a> on October 9, 2009, this time to commemorate the team's 50th anniversary season in Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-327"><a href="#cite_note-327">[327]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>In the first seven years in Los Angeles, the team played their home games at the <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Memorial_Sports_Arena" title="Los Angeles Memorial Sports Arena">Los Angeles Memorial Sports Arena</a>, south of Downtown Los Angeles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-328"><a href="#cite_note-328">[328]</a></sup> While the team played in Minneapolis, the team played their home games at the <a href="/wiki/Minneapolis_Auditorium" title="Minneapolis Auditorium">Minneapolis Auditorium</a> from 1947 to 1960.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-329"><a href="#cite_note-329">[329]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The Lakers hold the draft rights to the following unsigned draft picks who have been playing outside the NBA.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-330"><a href="#cite_note-330">[330]</a></sup> A drafted player, either an international draftee or a college draftee who is not signed by the team that drafted him, is allowed to sign with any non-NBA teams. In this case, the team retains the player's draft rights in the NBA until one year after the player's contract with the non-NBA team ends.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-331"><a href="#cite_note-331">[331]</a></sup> This list includes draft rights that were acquired from trades with other teams.

<p>The Lakers have had three <a href="/wiki/List_of_first_overall_NBA_draft_picks" title="List of first overall NBA draft picks">first overall picks</a> in their history: Elgin Baylor (selected in <a href="/wiki/1958_NBA_draft" title="1958 NBA draft">1958</a>), Magic Johnson (selected in <a href="/wiki/1979_NBA_draft" title="1979 NBA draft">1979</a>) and James Worthy (selected in <a href="/wiki/1982_NBA_draft" title="1982 NBA draft">1982</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_Draft_History_334-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_Draft_History-334">[334]</a></sup> The Lakers have also had six <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NBA_Draft_Lottery" title="NBA Draft Lottery">lottery</a> picks in their history: Eddie Jones (selected 10th overall in <a href="/wiki/1994_NBA_draft" title="1994 NBA draft">1994</a>), Andrew Bynum (selected 10th overall in 2005), Julius Randle (selected 7th overall in 2014), D'Angelo Russell (selected 2nd overall in 2015), Brandon Ingram (selected 2nd overall in 2016), and Lonzo Ball (selected 2nd overall in 2017).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_Draft_History_334-1"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_Draft_History-334">[334]</a></sup> Other draft picks include Jerry West and Gail Goodrich in the 1960s, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Cooper" title="Michael Cooper">Michael Cooper</a> and <a href="/wiki/Norm_Nixon" title="Norm Nixon">Norm Nixon</a> in the 1970s, <a href="/wiki/A._C._Green" title="A. C. Green">A. C. Green</a> and <a href="/wiki/Vlade_Divac" title="Vlade Divac">Vlade Divac</a> in the 1980s, <a href="/wiki/Elden_Campbell" title="Elden Campbell">Elden Campbell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nick_Van_Exel" title="Nick Van Exel">Nick Van Exel</a>, Derek Fisher, and <a href="/wiki/Devean_George" title="Devean George">Devean George</a> in the 1990s, and <a href="/wiki/Luke_Walton" title="Luke Walton">Luke Walton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sasha_Vuja%C4%8Di%C4%87" title="Sasha Vujačić">Sasha Vujačić</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ronny_Turiaf" title="Ronny Turiaf">Ronny Turiaf</a> in the 2000s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakers_Draft_History_334-2"><a href="#cite_note-Lakers_Draft_History-334">[334]</a></sup>

<p>There have been 22 head coaches for the Lakers franchise. <a href="/wiki/John_Kundla" title="John Kundla">John Kundla</a> coached the team in Minneapolis when they won their first five <a href="/wiki/NBA_Finals" title="NBA Finals">BAA/NBA championships</a> from <a href="/wiki/1949_BAA_Finals" title="1949 BAA Finals">1949</a> to <a href="/wiki/1954_NBA_Finals" title="1954 NBA Finals">1954</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-History_of_the_Lakers_#_10_44-1"><a href="#cite_note-History_of_the_Lakers_#_10-44">[44]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Pat_Riley" title="Pat Riley">Pat Riley</a> is second in franchise history in both regular season and playoff games coached and wins.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley_Stats_335-0"><a href="#cite_note-Riley_Stats-335">[335]</a></sup> Phil Jackson broke Riley's regular season wins record in 2009, and he passed Riley's playoff wins and games coached records in 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley_Stats_335-1"><a href="#cite_note-Riley_Stats-335">[335]</a></sup> Jackson, Riley, Kundla, and Bill Sharman have all been inducted into the Basketball Hall of Fame for their coaching careers. <a href="/wiki/George_Mikan" title="George Mikan">George Mikan</a>, Jim Pollard, <a href="/wiki/Jerry_West" title="Jerry West">Jerry West</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pat_Riley" title="Pat Riley">Pat Riley</a>, Magic Johnson, <a href="/wiki/Kurt_Rambis" title="Kurt Rambis">Kurt Rambis</a>, <a href="/wiki/Byron_Scott" title="Byron Scott">Byron Scott</a> and <a href="/wiki/Luke_Walton" title="Luke Walton">Luke Walton</a> have all played and head coached for the Lakers. Jackson, who had two stints as head coach, was coach from 2005 to 2011. In May 2011, <a href="/wiki/Mike_Brown_(basketball,_born_1970)" title="Mike Brown (basketball, born 1970)">Mike Brown</a> was named his replacement for the 2011–12 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-336"><a href="#cite_note-336">[336]</a></sup> Brown was fired<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-337"><a href="#cite_note-337">[337]</a></sup> on November 9, 2012, after a 1–4 start. Assistant coach <a href="/wiki/Bernie_Bickerstaff" title="Bernie Bickerstaff">Bernie Bickerstaff</a> served as interim head coach for five games before the Lakers selected <a href="/wiki/Mike_D%27Antoni" title="Mike D'Antoni">Mike D'Antoni</a> as their new head coach. D'Antoni resigned at the end of the 2013–14 season. In July 2014, Byron Scott was hired as head coach.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-338"><a href="#cite_note-338">[338]</a></sup> After the 2015–16 season ended, Scott was fired. On April 29, 2016, former Lakers player <a href="/wiki/Luke_Walton" title="Luke Walton">Luke Walton</a> was named as Scott's replacement,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-339"><a href="#cite_note-339">[339]</a></sup> and served as head coach until the end of the 2018–19 season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-340"><a href="#cite_note-340">[340]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Frank_Vogel" title="Frank Vogel">Frank Vogel</a> was named his successor on a multiyear deal announced on May 13, 2019.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-341"><a href="#cite_note-341">[341]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>In addition, several other players and coaches who were instrumental to the franchise's success during its days in Minneapolis were named Honored Minneapolis Lakers, although their numbers are not retired by the franchise:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-349"><a href="#cite_note-349">[349]</a></sup>

<p>For 41 years, Chick Hearn was the team's broadcaster until his death in 2002. He broadcast 3,338 consecutive games between November 21, 1965, and December 16, 2001.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-350"><a href="#cite_note-350">[350]</a></sup> Hearn came up with West's "Mr. Clutch" nickname.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-351"><a href="#cite_note-351">[351]</a></sup> He was a part of the team's "inner sanctum" when Cooke was owner, and was consulted on basketball decisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-352"><a href="#cite_note-352">[352]</a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sunderland" title="Paul Sunderland">Paul Sunderland</a>, who had filled in for a couple of games while Hearn recuperated in the <a href="/wiki/2001%E2%80%9302_NBA_season" title="2001–02 NBA season">2001–02 season</a>, was named the permanent <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Play-by-play" title="Play-by-play">play-by-play</a> announcer. <a href="/wiki/Stu_Lantz" title="Stu Lantz">Stu Lantz</a> was retained as the <a href="/wiki/Color_commentator" title="Color commentator">color commentator</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-353"><a href="#cite_note-353">[353]</a></sup> When Sunderland's contract expired in the summer of 2005, the team chose not to renew it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-354"><a href="#cite_note-354">[354]</a></sup> Then, <a href="/wiki/Joel_Meyers" title="Joel Meyers">Joel Meyers</a> moved in alongside Lantz as the television announcer, with <a href="/wiki/Spero_Dedes" title="Spero Dedes">Spero Dedes</a> and former Laker player <a href="/wiki/Mychal_Thompson" title="Mychal Thompson">Mychal Thompson</a> on the radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast_355-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast-355">[355]</a></sup>
</p>
<p>For the <a href="/wiki/2011%E2%80%9312_NBA_season" title="2011–12 NBA season">2011–12 season</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bill_Macdonald" title="Bill Macdonald">Bill Macdonald</a> became the new television play-by-play announcer, joining Lantz who remained as the color analyst. Meanwhile, <a href="/wiki/John_Ireland_(sportscaster)" title="John Ireland (sportscaster)">John Ireland</a> joined Mychal Thompson to call the games on radio.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-356"><a href="#cite_note-356">[356]</a></sup>

<p>Beginning in the <a href="/wiki/2009%E2%80%9310_NBA_season" title="2009–10 NBA season">2009–10 season</a>, Lakers radio broadcasts were heard on <a href="/wiki/KSPN_(AM)" title="KSPN (AM)">KSPN</a> (Los Angeles <a href="/wiki/ESPN_Radio" title="ESPN Radio">ESPN Radio</a> affiliate) in English and <a href="/wiki/KWKW" title="KWKW">KWKW</a> in Spanish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast_355-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast-355">[355]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast2_357-0"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast2-357">[357]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/KLAC_(AM)" title="KLAC (AM)">KLAC</a> had the team's radio broadcast rights from the <a href="/wiki/1976%E2%80%9377_NBA_season" title="1976–77 NBA season">1976–77 season</a> until the <a href="/wiki/2008%E2%80%9309_NBA_season" title="2008–09 NBA season">2008–09 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast2_357-1"><a href="#cite_note-Los_Angeles_Lakers_Broadcast2-357">[357]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-358"><a href="#cite_note-358">[358]</a></sup> Until 2011, telecasts had been split between <a href="/wiki/KCAL-TV" title="KCAL-TV">KCAL-TV</a> (road games) and Fox Sports West (home games), unless they are chosen for national broadcasts on <a href="/wiki/ESPN_on_ABC" title="ESPN on ABC">ABC</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schedule_359-0"><a href="#cite_note-schedule-359">[359]</a></sup> KCAL had been the Lakers' over-the-air television broadcaster since 1977, dating back to when the station was the <a href="/wiki/RKO_General" title="RKO General">RKO General</a>-owned KHJ-TV, the longest relationship between an NBA team and a television station. Prior to KHJ, Laker games were televised on <a href="/wiki/KTLA" title="KTLA">KTLA</a>. The Lakers had been on Fox Sports West since 1985, dating to when it was the original Prime Ticket and owned by Buss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-360"><a href="#cite_note-360">[360]</a></sup>

<p>On February 14, 2011, <a href="/wiki/Time_Warner_Cable" title="Time Warner Cable">Time Warner Cable</a> and the Lakers announced the formation of two new regional sports networks (one in English, one in Spanish) that would exclusively televise the team's games and related programming for 20 years starting with the <a href="/wiki/2012%E2%80%9313_NBA_season" title="2012–13 NBA season">2012–13 season</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-361"><a href="#cite_note-361">[361]</a></sup> The said networks were originally known as Time Warner Cable SportsNet, before it was renamed <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_SportsNet_(Los_Angeles)" title="Spectrum SportsNet (Los Angeles)">Spectrum SportsNet</a> in 2016 upon <a href="/wiki/Charter_Communications" title="Charter Communications">Charter Communications</a>' purchase of Time Warner Cable.
